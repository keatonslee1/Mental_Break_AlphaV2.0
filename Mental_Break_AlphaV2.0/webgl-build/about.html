Background

Policy school discipline meets techno-political storytelling.

Keaton Lee studied public policy at Harvard, philosophy at William & Mary, and computer science at TJHSST before founding Machinic Anarchy, the studio behind Mental Break.1

He treats the game as a living research lab: every narrative system is a thought experiment about how human-AI relationships could either reinforce or resist societal dysfunction.

Operating Premises

The studio roadmap is anchored by three beliefs that show up in every story branch.

Premise 01

Societal dysfunction is the central challenge.

Mental Break asks how late-stage corporate pressure and civic erosion affect the people trying to hold systems together.1

Premise 02

Human–AI relationships are fundamentally new.

Walker isn’t just code—she’s a mirror for the operators guiding her, and the game explores how intimacy shifts when automation pushes back.

Premise 03

The delta between good and bad outcomes is wide.

Every build experiments with aligning human-AI collaboration toward healthier futures rather than dystopian ones.1
